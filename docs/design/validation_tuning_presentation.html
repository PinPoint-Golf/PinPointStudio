Core Framework

Three Disciplines, Never Conflated

To prevent mathematical circularity, we enforce a strict separation between verification, validation, and calibration across our pipeline layers:

  • 1

    Verification: "Did we build the math right?"

    We run bit-identical golden tests, determinism checks, and closed-form synthetic models to ensure our C++ code has zero logical bugs before processing real signals.

  • 2

    Tuning (Calibration): "Which variables optimize performance?"

    We optimize parameters (e.g., filter gains, detection thresholds) strictly on isolated, designated Tuning partitions of our database.

  • 3

    Validation: "Did we build the right measurement?"

    The cardinal sin is reporting validation scores on data used for tuning. We evaluate our pipeline's accuracy using independent reference data that the tuning phase never touched.

Measurement Validity

The Validity Hierarchy

A biomechanical metric is meaningless without context. We establish trust by proving our metrics climb the classic measurement science ladder:

  • 1

    Face & Content Validity

    We verify that metrics match anatomical ranges (e.g., Ryu ROM limits: $\le 54^\circ$ Flex, $\le 60^\circ$ Ext) and fully cover wrist DOFs defined by the ISB 2005 standard.

  • 2

    Construct & Concurrent Validity

    We prove scripted swing faults separate cleanly (Construct), and establish agreement against an independent, simultaneous criterion reference—HackMotion (Concurrent).

  • 3

    External & Predictive Validity

    We validate that measurements generalize across different body shapes/swings (External), and ultimately forecast launch monitor flight results (Predictive).

Statistical Power

The Mathematics of Sample Size

Sample size is governed by the specific statistic we compute, not by the project phase. We determine our dataset sizes using strict statistical power formulas:

  • 1

    Proportions (Pass-Rates, Fault Recall)

    Using binomial power n = z²·p(1-p)/E², a $\pm5\%$ confidence margin ($E=0.05, p\approx0.9$) requires n = 138 swings. Furthermore, by the Rule of Three, catching $k$ consecutive faults yields a lower recall bound of only $1 - 3/k$ (e.g., 12/12 caught faults implies recall is $\ge 75\%$). To claim recall $\ge 95\%$, we need ~60 clean trials per fault.

  • 2

    Bland-Altman Agreement Limits (LoA)

    The standard error of our limits of agreement maps to SE ≈ 1.71·s / √n. With n = 50 paired swings, we size the half-width of our 95% LoA confidence interval to a tight $\pm0.48 \cdot s$ (where $s$ is the standard deviation of differences measured in the pilot).

  • 3

    Reliability Floor (SEM & MDC)

    Typical error (SEM) has a sampling coefficient of variation of CV ≈ 1 / √(2(n-1)). A baseline floor of n = 25–30 repeated swings bounds the typical error margin to a precision of $\pm14\%$.

Corpus 2 Study

The Concurrent Agreement Protocol

To establish concurrent validity, Corpus 2 co-captures swings using PinPoint and HackMotion. We analyze three statistical measures per axis, per phase:

  • 1

    Bland-Altman Limits of Agreement

    We report mean bias d = 1/n·∑dᵢ and 95% Limits of Agreement LoA = d ± 1.96·SD_diff. We map differences against average values to detect proportional bias or heteroscedasticity.

  • 2

    Intraclass Correlation (ICC(A,1))

    Correlation coefficients (r) can inflate values if a constant offset is present. We compute ICC(A,1)—the two-way mixed, single-measure absolute agreement coefficient—to strictly punish systematic shifts.

  • 3

    Pre-Registered Thresholds

    We enforce pre-declared boundaries: Flexion/Extension & Forearm Roll must satisfy RMSE ≤ 4–5°, ICC ≥ 0.90, Bias ±2°. Radial-Ulnar Hinge, bounded by the criterion's own noise floor (~5°), targets RMSE ≤ 8°.

Degrees of Freedom

Anatomical Alignment & Axis Leakage

Sensors are mounted to soft tissue, not bone. Minor physical misalignments introduce systematic cross-axis coordinate leaks:

  • !

    Anatomical Coordinate Transform

    We convert raw sensor rotations into anatomical joint angles using the transform matrix: q_anat = A·q_raw·M (where A is the forearm joint frame, and M is the sensor mount rotation). A $5^\circ$ error in sensor placement leaks flexion data directly into the radial-ulnar hinge reading.

  • !

    Static Calibration Pose

    To resolve the A·M alignment window, players execute a mandatory 1-second static address pose. The system runs a Kabsch yaw-alignment algorithm against the gravity vector to calculate and freeze correction offsets.

  • !

    Pronation-Supination Mismatch

    HackMotion calculates forearm roll using one glove sensor (inferring rotation). PinPoint measures roll directly using forearm + hand sensor pairs. Disagreements can be real anatomical details, not errors.

Filter Dynamics

Conquering Accelerometer Saturation

Golf swing dynamics violate the core assumption of orientation filters: that the accelerometer only measures gravity. We implement a dual-action correction:

  • 1

    Continuous Down-Weighted Accel Gain

    Sustained swing speed creates extreme linear forces. We continuously scale down the Madgwick gain $\beta$ (or inflate the ESKF covariance $R$) based on the accelerometer residual and gyroscope magnitude: e = |‖a‖ - 1g| and ‖ω‖. In high dynamics, we switch to pure gyro integration.

  • 2

    Discrete Impact-Blanking Window

    At ball strike, forces exceed the physical range of the sensor, saturating and clipping at $\pm16g$. Since the impact frame is back-dated, we freeze all accelerometer corrections across a narrow window: [impactUs - 15ms, impactUs + 30ms], avoiding shock wave contamination.

  • 3

    Yaw Observability Caveat

    Gravity vectors only calibrate pitch and roll. Wrist yaw (rotation in the horizontal plane) is unobservable via accelerometers and accumulates drift. Yaw is locked using still-window Kabsch re-alignment.

Vision Tracking

Motion Blur & Wedge Integration

At 100+ mph swing speeds, the club shaft does not appear as a solid line on camera. We bypass motion blur limits using dynamic wedge integration:

  • 1

    Exposure-Based Fan Width Calculation

    Instead of thresholding a thin line, the camera module calculates the expected angular swept wedge width: Δθ ≈ ω · t_exp (where ω is the clubhead angular velocity, and t_exp is the exposure time).

  • 2

    Swept Energy Summing

    Our algorithm sums faint pixel intensities across the predicted fan path. While single pixels are faint and noisy, the aggregated path energy provides mathematically robust tracking verification.

  • 3

    Arm-Scale Constraints (R1 & R5)

    Standard search algorithms panic if feet are cropped. We measure the pixel length of the player's arm (Shoulder-to-Wrist) to set a dynamic search radius ($R_{max} \approx 2.1 \times Arm$). Any candidate lines shorter than one arm length are rejected to filter out watch bands or clothing folds.

Population Statistics

The Clustered Data Risk

Swings captured from a single golfer are highly correlated. Treating them as independent observations for population claims is a fatal statistical error:

  • !

    The Design Effect (Deff) Inflation

    Repeated measures carry a design effect: Deff = 1 + (m-1)·ρ (where m is swings per player, and ρ is the intra-golfer correlation). If a single golfer repeats 50 swings and the intra-golfer correlation is high ($\rho \approx 0.5$), the design effect is 25.5. This reduces our effective sample size from 50 to only 1.96 independent swings!

  • !

    Within-Subject vs. Between-Subject Agreement

    Corpora 1 & 2 validate within-subject agreement (sensor/mount consistency). We cannot make population-level accuracy claims before Corpus 3, which collects data from 8–12 different golfers to account for physical diversity.

  • ✓

    The Multi-Golfer Floor

    By evaluating 8–12 independent golfers, we measure true between-subject anatomical and sensor-mounting variances. This allows us to publish a statistically robust, generalizable accuracy standard.

Governance

Governance & Sweep Optimization

Tuning our ~34 variables against small samples risks severe overfitting. We apply mathematical controls and parameter sweeps to govern code changes:

  • 1

    Data Partitioning (Tune, Val, Held-out)

    Sweeps search for parameters strictly on the Tuning partition. The selection is cross-validated on Validation, and the final parameters are evaluated exactly once on the locked Held-out set at freeze.

  • 2

    Optimization Algorithms

    We match the optimiser to the mathematical surface: Coordinate Descent for independent variables, Bayesian Optimization (TPE/GP) for complex coordinate interactions, and CMA-ES for continuous, non-separable filter gain tables.

  • 3

    Double-Gated Objectives

    During sweeps, we apply a soft regression penalty to keep search algorithms from deadlocking. At final code freeze, we apply a hard gate: Regressions = 0. Every default parameter in pp_tuned_constants.h must improve average scores without breaking a single existing swing.
