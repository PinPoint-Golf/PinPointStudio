Innovation Spotlight

Bringing Light to the Blur

How PinPointStudio uses anatomy, physics, and kinematics to track a golf club shaft in high-speed motion

A Non-Technical Deep Dive: Learn how we solved the hardest challenge in golf tracking—spotting a dark 1/4-inch stick moving at 100+ mph against busy backgrounds.

The Problem

The Fastest Move in Sports

A golf swing lasts less than two seconds. The critical downswing takes just a fraction of a second. This speed introduces severe issues for standard camera tracking:

  • !

    Severe Motion Blur

    At 100+ mph, a thin golf shaft turns into a semi-transparent, faint gray smear (or wedge) on camera, rather than a solid line.

  • !

    Background Clutter

    Vertical alignment sticks on the ground, wrinkles in trousers, shadows, and seams on turf confuse the algorithm into "detecting" a club where none exists.

Baseline Math

How the Computer Looks

The base algorithm does not scan the whole screen at once; it works outward from what it already knows:

  • 1

    Anchor on the Hands

    First, it locates the golfer's hands using AI body-pose detection. This acts as the pivot center.

  • 2

    Radial Search (Rays)

    It projects search lines (rays) outward in a 360-degree circle from the hands up to a maximum radius.

  • 3

    Ridge Profiling

    Along each ray, it looks for high-contrast, linear dark pixels. A long, solid sequence of dark pixels indicates a candidate shaft.

The Blind Spots

Where the Old Way Failed

Because the tracker was blind to the golfer's anatomy, it fell into two classic traps:

  • ✗

    Cropping Collapse (G1)

    To estimate sizing, the system guessed the golfer was 1.70 meters tall. But if the camera cropped out the feet, the system panicked, expanded the search region to the whole screen, and got lost in background noise.

  • ✗

    Obsession with Tiny Lines (G4)

    A club is physically longer than a human arm. Yet the algorithm would lock onto a 2-inch sleeve crease or watch band next to the hands and confidently declare it a "shaft" because it was bright and high-contrast.

Enhancements R1 & R5

Scaling and Gating by the Arm

We solved these errors by linking the shaft search directly to the skeleton's arm geometry:

  • ✓

    R1: Arm-Based Sizing (No Crop Panic)

    Instead of guessing height, we measure the pixel length of the arm (shoulder-to-wrist). Since a club is always ~1.8 to 2.2 times arm length, we set a precise maximum search radius, no matter how the video is cropped.

  • ✓

    R5: The Physical-Length Gate

    A club is physically longer than a human arm. The system now enforces a strict rule: any detection shorter than one arm length is rejected instantly. Sleeve folds and watches are filtered out before they can confuse the pathfinder.

Enhancement R6

The Double-Pendulum Model

A golf swing behaves mechanically like a two-link double pendulum. We use this physics-based constraint to create a dynamic angle guardrail:

  • ✓

    Predictable Wrist-Cock Angle

    The angle between the lead arm and the club follows a strict biomechanical curve based on the phase of the swing (e.g., 0° at address, ~90° at the top, holding lag, releasing to 0° at impact).

  • ✓

    Dynamic Angle Guardrail

    Knowing the swing phase and lead-arm angle, the system predicts where the club *should* be. It places a soft sector around this angle and hard-rejects anything grossly violating the model.

Enhancement R8

Don't Fight the Blur, Predict It

In the fastest downswing phase, the club is a ghost. We stopped looking for a crisp straight line and rewrote the rules for the high-speed zone:

  • ✓

    Wedge Integration

    Instead of trying to threshold a thin line (which fails because the line is blurred out), the system calculates the swept fan width based on club head speed ($\omega$) and exposure time ($t_{\text{exp}}$).

  • ✓

    Swept Energy Summing

    The system sums the faint image energy across the predicted fan. A single pixel is faint, but the aggregate energy of the sweep is strong enough to mathematically prove the club is there.

Replay Overlay & QA

Replay and Self-Diagnosis

To visualize and test these improvements, we built a dual-track output and diagnostic overlay:

  • 1

    Actual vs. Predicted Output (R7)

    The system computes two paths: Actual (derived from the pixels) and Predicted (derived from the physics model). Comparing them yields a "residual error" indicating tracker health.

  • 2

    Ghost Overlay Mode

    For QA and testing, developers can overlay a semi-transparent, dashed "ghost" line of the physics prediction behind the solid detected club line.

  • 3

    Glowing Clubhead Trail

    Replay renderings feature a beautifully faded, glowing trail of the last 10 clubhead positions to help visualize swing path and release.

Completed Upgrades

Implemented Accuracy Upgrades

We have successfully implemented and verified the four approved accuracy recommendations:

  • 1

    Dynamic Exposure Calibration (Rec 1)

    Instead of a fixed 3 ms default, the system runs a pre-pass to measure observed wedge widths over moderate swing-rate frames, fitting the camera exposure time $t_{\text{exp}}$ dynamically.

  • 2

    Residual-Bounded Aggressiveness (Rec 2)

    Calculates the prior-free tracking residual in the pre-pass to automatically scale prior widths and relax blur detection thresholds for custom/non-standard stances.

  • 3

    Chirality Lock & Handedness (Rec 3)

    Autodetects left/right-handedness and mirror flips from initial displacement trajectories during the backswing, locking swing direction safely.

  • 4

    Temporal Background Subtraction (Rec 4)

    Estimates a median background to isolate motion, subtracting it dynamically and masking out the moving golfer's body skeleton to maximize blur-smear tracking sensitivity.

Next Horizon

Roadmap to 99%+ Accuracy

To push the boundaries of tracking accuracy further, we recommend the following next steps:

  • 1

    3D Swing-Plane Projection (Rec 5)

    Use camera intrinsics to project the 3D wrist-cock prior onto the 2D camera coordinate plane to resolve perspective distortions and foreshortening issues.

  • 2

    Corpus-Wide Calibration (K5)

    * Run validation A/B sweeps against empirical IMU data to refine kinematic tables ($q_{\text{Hand}}$ angular offsets).
  • 3

    Down-The-Line (DTL) Support

    Extend the double-pendulum constraint equations to support down-the-line camera setups with modified angular priors.
