How We Analyze

The Nine-Layer Swing Pipeline

To map your swing in milliseconds, PinPointStudio runs your shot through a sequential, nine-stage analysis process:

  • 1

    AI Pose & Tracker (Layers 0-1)

    Extracts raw frames and runs high-precision AI models (ViTPose-L/H) to trace 133 joint landmarks on your body, hands, and fingers, while tracking the clubhead and ball.

  • 2

    3D Reconstruction & Fusion (Layers 2-3)

    Triangulates camera angles and fuses them with 100 Hz IMU (motion sensor) quaternions. This maps your rotations in a virtual metric 3D space, resolving occlusion.

  • 3

    Anatomical Skeleton & Scores (Layers 4-8)

    Smoothes joint paths with Kalman/RTS filters, fits them to a stable physical skeleton, anchors events on impact, computes scores, and ranks faults with targeted drills.

Power Generation

The Power Coil: X-Factor

Speed in golf isn't about arm strength; it is about building elastic tension in your core, which we measure in degrees of separation:

  • 1

    X-Factor (The Backswing Coil)

    The difference in rotation between your shoulders (thorax) and hips (pelvis) at the top of the backswing. A strong coil separates the two axes by 40° to 50°, stretching the core muscles.

  • 2

    X-Factor Stretch (The Downswing Sling)

    As the downswing begins, the hips start turning *first*, while the shoulders remain coiled. This increases the separation briefly, creating an extra stretch (ideal is +5°) that acts like a released slingshot.

  • 3

    How to use it

    If your X-Factor is low, focus on hip stability. Letting your hips drift or turn too much limits the coil. If your stretch is low, focus on starting your downswing from the ground up, not with your upper body.

Swing Timing

The Kinematic Sequence

Like cracking a whip, speed in a golf swing travels through your body parts in a highly specific sequence:

  • 1

    The Proper Order

    Energy must peak and transfer from bottom to top: Hips (Pelvis) → Chest (Thorax) → Lead Arm → Club. Each section acts as a pivot, passing speed forward.

  • 2

    Energy Peak & Deceleration

    To transfer speed, a segment must rapidly decelerate. When your hips brake, that energy surges into your chest; when your chest brakes, it snaps into your arms.

  • 3

    The "Over the Top" Fault

    If your shoulders or arms peak before your hips (out of order), you are "casting." This destroys clubhead speed, changes the swing path, and causes slices.

Clubface Control

Lead Wrist: Flexion & Extension

The direction of your shot is dictated by the clubface angle, which is controlled directly by your lead wrist position:

  • 1

    Flexion (Bowed / Palm Down)

    Bowing the wrist forward toward your palm. This rotates the clubface closed. If you struggle with slicing, increasing flexion will help square or close the face.

  • 2

    Extension (Cupped / Knuckles Up)

    Bending the wrist backward like you're cup-handling. This rotates the clubface open. Excessive cupping is the number one cause of high, weak slices.

  • 3

    Radial / Ulnar Hinge

    Cocking the wrist up (radial) creates the angle that stores "lag" during the downswing. Releasing it down (ulnar) snaps the club straight at impact.

Stability & Posture

Body Stability: Sway, Thrust & Lift

Consistency requires rotating around a solid spine angle without swaying side-to-side or thrusting your hips toward the ball:

  • 1

    Sway (Side-to-Side Drift)

    Lateral movement of your hips. You want a small shift away from the target in the backswing, followed by a strong drive *toward* the target on the downswing.

  • 2

    Thrust (Early Extension)

    Hips moving toward the ball. This is a massive fault: it crowds your arms, forces you to stand up out of your posture, and causes blocks or shanks.

  • 3

    Lift (Up & Down)

    Vertical displacement. Keeping your height stable through backswing, then pushing off the ground at impact (controlled vertical lift) creates power.

Scoring Math

Adherence Score: The Weakest Link

For Swing and GRF sessions, we do not use simple averages. We score using a **weighted geometric mean** so a major error cannot be hidden:

  • 1

    Non-Compensatory Scoring

    If you have five perfect metrics (100) and one critical fault like massive early extension (20), a simple average would reward you with a good 86 score.

  • 2

    Geometric Mean Penalty

    By multiplying scores together and taking the root, a single low number pulls the entire score down. Your final score becomes a realistic 40.

  • 3

    Coaching Philosophy

    You cannot compensate for a critical mechanical fault by making another part of your swing "perfect." The score keeps you honest about what to fix.

Style Diagnostics

Wrist Resemblance: Your Swing Style

Unlike body posture, there is no single "correct" wrist shape. Great players swing differently. PinPointStudio diagnoses your unique style:

  • 1

    Archetype Resemblance

    We evaluate your wrist angles at the top of the backswing and at impact, matching you to three styles: **Bowed** (closed/strong face), **Neutral**, or **Cupped** (open/weak face).

  • 2

    Resemblance Match (0-100)

    You receive independent scores for each archetype. Your highest score designates your pattern. A high score means you are consistent with that specific style.

  • 3

    Blended Badge

    If your top two resemblance scores are within 10 points, you get a "Blended" badge. This means your wrist movements are hybrid. Focus on stabilizing one style rather than blending styles.

Data Trust

Confidence & Score Intervals

Tracking devices operate in the real world where shadows and noise exist. We communicate this using an honest confidence range:

  • 1

    The "82 (±9)" Interval

    Instead of a single arbitrary score, we present your result as an interval (e.g. 73 to 91, centered at 82). This represents the mathematical margin of timing and sensor noise.

  • 2

    Data Quality Dictates the Width

    Perfect lighting, snug sensor straps, and dual calibrated cameras result in narrow intervals (e.g. ±3). Poor lighting or loose straps widen it (e.g. ±12).

  • 3

    No Penalty for Noise

    Crucially, **lower confidence only widens the interval; it never lowers your score**. We protect your performance history from environment glitches.

Action Plan

How to Use the Data to Improve

Don't try to fix everything at once. Use PinPointStudio's prioritized step-by-step method to change your swing:

  • 1

    Identify Your Style first

    Check your Wrist Resemblance. If you are a bowed-wrist player, do not copy neutral or cupped wrist tips—consistency within your style is the goal.

  • 2

    Attack the Top-Ranked Fault

    The AI Coach ranks faults based on points lost (using the geometric mean). The top fault is what's holding your score back the most. Focus only on that one.

  • 3

    Execute the Drill & Re-Test

    Every ranked fault has a plain-English drill. Work on the drill, capture a new swing, and monitor the specific metric drift toward green.
